❌ Common Mistakes: Casual vs Business

What NOT to say in professional settings

🗣️ Mistakes to Avoid (8)

1. "Gonna" vs "Going to" ›
❌ Casual: "I'm gonna go."
✅ Business: "I'm going to attend."
Rule: Never use "gonna" in professional writing/speaking
2. "Wanna" vs "Want to" ›
❌ Casual: "Do you wanna join?"
✅ Business: "Would you like to join?"
Rule: Use formal "want to" in work contexts
3. "You guys" vs "You all" ›
❌ Casual: "You guys ready?"
✅ Business: "Is everyone ready?"
Rule: Avoid "guys" in plural; use "everyone" or "team"
4. "Ain't" vs "Isn't/Aren't" ›
❌ Casual: "It ain't happening."
✅ Business: "It isn't happening."
Rule: "Ain't" is always incorrect in formal English
5. "Kinda" vs "Kind of" ›
❌ Casual: "It's kinda complicated."
✅ Business: "It's somewhat complicated."
Rule: Write out full words; don't shorten to "kinda/sorta"
6. Double negatives ›
❌ Casual: "I ain't got no problem."
✅ Business: "I don't have any problems."
Rule: Never use two negatives together
7. Starting with "So..." ›
❌ Casual: "So like, we should go."
✅ Business: "Therefore, we should proceed."
Rule: Don't start sentences with "so" in formal writing
8. Slang abbreviations ›
❌ Casual: "IDK what 2 do."
✅ Business: "I don't know what to do."
Rule: Write out full words in professional emails

📚 Grammar - Verb Tenses
⭐ Most Used in Business English
1. Simple Present
When & Why: Use for current facts, daily routines, job descriptions, permanent situations, and general truths
How: + s/es for
Why: The foundation of English. Use it to describe habitual actions, facts about your job, and timeless truths.
Business Context: Job interviews ("I manage a team of 5"), company descriptions ("We specialize in software"), daily duties ("I attend meetings every Monday")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use time expressions: always, usually, every day, on Mondays, often, rarely
✓ "We have meetings every Tuesday" = regular habit/routine (YES, Simple Present!)
✗ Don't use for one-time past events ("Yesterday I wrote a report" - use Past Simple)
More Examples:
• I work in marketing. (job description)
• She manages the team. (responsibility/role)
• The company produces software worldwide. (business fact)
• We have meetings every Tuesday. (regular routine)
• My boss attends client calls twice a week. (repeated action)
• The office opens at 9 AM. (daily fact)
• They usually send invoices on Fridays. (regular habit)
• I check emails every morning. (daily routine)
• Sarah represents the company at conferences. (responsibility)
• We follow best practices in all projects. (general truth)

4. Who manages your team?
5. What responsibilities do you have in your job?

2. Simple Past
When & Why: Use for completed actions that happened at a specific time in the past
How: + -ed (or )
Why: Essential for storytelling, reporting past results, and discussing completed projects. The most common past tense in business conversation.
Business Context: Meeting reports ("I finished the analysis yesterday"), project retrospectives ("We launched in February"), interview questions ("Tell me about a challenge you solved")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use time expressions: yesterday, last week, in 2024, at 3 PM
✓ Regular: work→worked, complete→completed | Irregular: go→went, write→written
✗ Don't use for ongoing past actions ("I was working when..." - use Past Continuous)
I completed the report yesterday. She presented the findings last Monday. We launched the product in 2024. They hired five new employees last month.
More Examples:
• I finished the analysis yesterday. (one-time past event)
• She sent the email on Friday. (completed action)
• We met the deadline last week. (past achievement)
• The client approved the budget. (past decision)
• I worked on that project for 2 months. (finished period)
• They increased sales by 25% last quarter. (past result)

3. Present Perfect
When & Why: Use for past experiences that are still relevant now, recent achievements, or things that started in the past and may continue
How: Have/Has +
Why: Connects the past to the present. Perfect for showing career experience, accomplishments, and the impact of past actions on current situation.
Business Context: CVs and resumes ("I have managed 5 projects"), status updates ("We have completed phase 1"), job interviews ("I have worked in 3 countries")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use: ever, never, for (4 years), since (January)
✓ Don't give a specific time that's finished: ❌ "I have worked there last year" → ✓ "I worked there last year"
✓ The exact time may not matter: "I have completed the report" (good for status)
I have worked here for 5 years. She has completed three projects this quarter. We have reviewed the proposal. Have you ever worked abroad? They have never missed a deadline.
More Examples:
• I have managed 10+ projects. (career achievement)
• She has been with the company since 2020. (ongoing tenure)
• We have increased productivity by 30%. (current result of past action)
• I have never missed a deadline. (life experience)
• They have already submitted the report. (recent completion)
• I have worked in 3 different departments. (career experience)
• She has led multiple successful campaigns. (professional achievements)
• We have completed phase 1 of the project. (recent accomplishment)
• Have you ever presented to executives? (life experience question)

4. Present Continuous
When & Why: Use for actions happening RIGHT NOW or temporary situations that are ongoing
How: Am/Is/Are +
Why: Shows what's currently happening. Great for status updates, real-time reporting, and describing temporary situations.
Business Context: Quick status messages ("I'm attending a meeting"), email updates ("We are finalizing the budget"), describing current projects ("She is leading the new initiative")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use: right now, at the moment, currently, today, this week
✓ Can also show temporary situations: "I'm working from home this month"
✗ Don't use for permanent situations ("I work in marketing" - use Simple Present)
I am writing the report right now. She is attending the client meeting at the moment. We are discussing budget cuts today. Are you working on the new project?
More Examples:

5. Future Simple (Will)
When & Why: Use for future plans, predictions, promises, decisions made at the moment, or general future facts
How: Will +
Why: Shows reliability, commitment, and involves less planning than "going to." Common in formal promises and professional commitments.
Business Context: Commitments ("I will send you the report tomorrow"), promises ("We will meet the deadline"), predictions ("Sales will increase next quarter"), new decisions ("I'll take this client")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use: tomorrow, next week, in the future, later
✓ "Will" shows a promise or decision made NOW: "I will help you" (I decide right now)
✓ Different from "going to": "I'll go" (decision now) vs "I'm going to go" (already planned)
I will send the email tomorrow. We will increase production next quarter. She will lead the new project. The company will expand to 3 new markets this year.
More Examples:

• She will attend the conference next month. (future plan)

6. Past Perfect
When & Why: Use when explaining sequence - showing that one past action happened BEFORE another past action
How: Had +
Why: Clarifies order of events and prevents confusion about what happened when. Useful in detailed storytelling.
Business Context: Incident reports ("When the system failed, we had already backed up the data"), project timelines ("By the time she arrived, the team had already started"), client issues ("The client had submitted the budget before we requested it")
💡 Tips:
✓ Use "had" for the FIRST Past Perfect, then "did" for the second: "I had finished (first) when she arrived (second)"
✓ Time words: before, by the time, after, already
✓ Often used with "when" or "by the time"
I had finished the report before the meeting started. By the time she arrived, the team had already completed the analysis. We had reviewed the budget when the CFO called. After we had signed the contract, they sent new terms.
More Examples:
• The client had approved before we sent it. (first past event)
• I had worked for 3 companies before this one. (sequence of past events)
• She had already left when I arrived at the office. (two past moments)
• They had completed the project before the deadline passed. (two past achievements)
• I had submitted the proposal before the client called. (order of events)
• We had backed up the data before the system crashed. (two related past events)
• By the time the meeting ended, they had reached an agreement. (both in past)
• She had managed five projects before taking this role. (past experience)
• When the crisis started, we had already prepared. (two past moments)
• I had trained 10 employees before the restructuring happened. (two sequential past events)

7. Present Perfect Continuous
When & Why: Use to emphasize HOW LONG something has been happening from past to now
How: Have/Has + been +
Why: Focuses on the duration, effort, or continuation. Shows ongoing effort and commitment.
Business Context: Status updates emphasizing effort ("I've been working on this for months"), demonstrating experience ("He's been managing the team since 2020"), explaining ongoing projects
💡 Tips:
✓ Emphasis on DURATION: "I've been working for 3 months" (shows effort)
✓ Difference: Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous:
- "I have completed 5 projects" (focus: how many - results)
- "I have been working for 5 hours" (focus: how long - effort)
✓ Use: for, since
I have been working on this project for 3 months. She has been managing that account since January. We have been discussing this contract all week. How long have you been in your current role?

8. Past Continuous
When & Why: Use for actions that were IN PROGRESS when something else happened, or two things happening at the same time in the past
How: Was/Were +
Why: Helps explain context. Show what was happening as background to another past event.
Business Context: Explaining interruptions ("I was presenting when the fire alarm went off"), incident reports ("The system was processing when it crashed"), meeting situations ("We were discussing the budget when...")
💡 Tips:
✓ Usually used WITH Simple Past: "I was working (continuous) when she called (simple past)"
✓ The Past Continuous provides the background/context
✓ Structure: "While I was doing X, Y happened"
✓ Less common than Simple Past, but important for detailed narratives
I was working when the power went out. She was presenting when the client called. We were discussing the budget when they announced the layoffs. While I was reviewing the files, I found the error.
More Examples:
• I was writing the report when the system crashed. (interrupted action)
• She was presenting when the client interrupted. (background action)
• We were meeting when the fire alarm went off. (background/context)
• They were negotiating when unexpected news arrived. (when + past event)
• I was reviewing the data while you were preparing the slides. (two simultaneous past actions)
• He was training new staff when the reorganization was announced. (background action)
• We were implementing changes when the budget cuts came. (concurrent past events)
• She was working on the contract when the legal team called. (interrupted progress)
• I was speaking with the CEO when my phone died. (background + interruption)
• They were discussing the strategy when the board walked in. (background/context)

9. Going To (Future Plan)
When & Why: Use for plans made BEFORE NOW, intentions, and near future events that are already planned
How: Am/Is/Are + going to +
Why: Shows a clear plan or arrangement. More casual than "will" and suggests planning that already happened.
Business Context: Announcing plans ("We're going to launch next month"), discussing schedule ("I'm going to meet with the client tomorrow"), informal commitments, intention statements
💡 Tips:
✓ "Going to" = plan made before now | "Will" = decision made right now
✓ "I'm going to leave" (already decided) vs "I will leave" (deciding now)
✓ Can show prediction based on present evidence: "Look at the storm - it's going to rain"
✓ More casual/conversational than "will"
I am going to call the client later. We are going to launch next month. She is going to take on a leadership role. Are you going to attend the conference?

• He is going to work remotely from now on. (new plan)
• They are going to cancel the conference. (intention based on current situation)

10. Future Perfect
When & Why: Use to show something WILL BE COMPLETED by a specific time in the future
How: Will + have +
Why: Emphasizes deadline and completion. Shows commitment to finishing by a specific date.
Business Context: Deadlines ("I will have finished the report by Friday"), project planning ("We will have completed testing by month-end"), commitment statements ("The team will have delivered the project by Q2")
💡 Tips:
✓ Rarely used but VERY important for professional deadlines
✓ Shows: Future point + completion BEFORE that point
✓ Use: by (a deadline), by Friday, by next month, by the end of the quarter
✓ "I will have finished" = completion guaranteed by that time
I will have finished the analysis by Friday. She will have completed testing by month-end. We will have launched the new product by Q3. By next year, the company will have expanded to 5 countries.
More Examples:
