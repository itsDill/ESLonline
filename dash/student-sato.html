⚖️ Formal vs. Casual English

Know when and how to adjust your language for different situations

Greetings: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "Good morning, it's a pleasure to meet you."
CASUAL: "Hey! How's it going?"
WHEN TO USE: Formal = First meetings, interviews, clients. Casual = Colleagues you know, friends
Making Requests: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "Would you be able to assist me with this report?"
CASUAL: "Can you help me with this?"
WHEN TO USE: Formal = New contacts, important requests. Casual = Team members, friends
Expressing Disagreement: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "I respectfully disagree with that approach."
CASUAL: "I don't think that's the best way to do it."
WHEN TO USE: Formal = Clients, seniors. Casual = Peers, trusted colleagues
Saying "I Don't Know": Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "I'm not certain about that. Let me look into it."
CASUAL: "I have no idea. Let me check."
WHEN TO USE: Formal = Presentations, client calls. Casual = Team meeting, quick chat
Describing a Problem: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "We encountered some technical difficulties with the system."
CASUAL: "The system is broken. It's driving me crazy."
WHEN TO USE: Formal = Reports, emails to management. Casual = Team slack, hallway chats
Email Closings: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "Best regards, Sarah Johnson"
CASUAL: "Cheers, Sarah" or "Thanks! Sarah"
HINT: When in doubt, use formal. You can't go wrong being too professional.
Thanking Someone: Formal vs Casual ›
FORMAL: "Thank you for your consideration. I appreciate your time."
CASUAL: "Thanks so much! Really appreciate it!"
WHEN TO USE: Formal = Interviews, after meetings with important contacts. Casual = Everyday team interactions
📚 Common Mistakes & Learning Tips

Avoid these common errors and boost your English skills

❌ "I have told you" vs ✅ "I told you" ›
Mistake: Using present perfect when you should use past tense
Rule: When the time is stated (yesterday, last week), use simple past
Correct: "I told you about this yesterday."
❌ "I am agree" vs ✅ "I agree" ›
Mistake: Adding "am" to action verbs
Rule: Don't use "be" with action verbs
Remember: Agree, understand, like, enjoy, want don't need "am/is/are"
❌ "Interesting" vs ✅ "Interested" ›
Confusing? Here's the trick:
-ing: The thing/person IS like that (The project is interesting)
-ed: The person FEELS that way (I am interested in the project)
❌ Mixing up "Since" and "For" ›
SINCE: A point in time (2020, Monday, last year)
FOR: A period/duration (2 years, 3 hours, since morning)
Correct: "I've worked here since 2020." "I've worked here for 4 years."
❌ "I will be happy if..." vs ✅ "I would be happy if..." ›
Mistake: Using "will" in conditional sentences
Rule: Use would/if for hypothetical situations
Correct: "I would be happy if you joined us." (polite, conditional)
