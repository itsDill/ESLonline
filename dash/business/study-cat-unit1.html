📐 Grammar — Present Simple: be & have ▼

Part 1: The verb be

Pronoun Positive Negative Short form
I I am a manager. I am not a teacher. I'm / I'm not
You You are Thai. You are not British. You're / You're not
He / She / It She is the director. He is not here. She's / He's not
We / They They are engineers. We are not free. They're / We're not
Questions with be: Move the verb before the subject.
Are you from Thailand? → Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.
Is she the manager? → Yes, she is. / No, she isn't.
What is your name? → My name is Cat.
Where are you from? → I'm from Thailand.
Are you a teacher? → Yes, I am. / No, I'm not — I'm an accountant.

Part 2: The verb have

Pronoun Positive Negative
I / You / We / They I have a business card. I don't have your number.
He / She / It She has a meeting at 3pm. He doesn't have an office.
Questions with have: Use do / does.
Do you have a business card? → Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
Does she have your number? → Yes, she does. / No, she doesn't.
Do you have an email address? → Yes, I do. It's cat@company.com
Does he have the report? → No, he doesn't. I'll send it later.

Part 3: Wh- Questions

Question word Example question Short answer pattern
What What do you do? I'm a manager at ABC Co.
Where Where are you from? I'm from Bangkok.
Who Who is your manager? Her name is Lisa.
How How are you? I'm fine, thank you.
⚠️ Common mistake: Don't say "What are you do?" — use do, not are for jobs.
✅ "What do you do?" (correct)  |  ❌ "What are you do?" (wrong)
💼 Vocabulary — Jobs, Companies & Countries ▼

Jobs & Titles

manager
someone who leads a team
"I'm a manager at a bank."
director
senior leader of a company or department
"She's the sales director."
engineer
designs or builds things
"He's a software engineer."
accountant
manages money and finances
"I'm an accountant."
consultant
gives expert advice
"She's an IT consultant."
assistant
helps someone with their work
"He's the director's assistant."
receptionist
greets visitors, answers phones
"She works as a receptionist."
HR manager
manages staff and recruitment
"He's in HR."

Company & Department Words

headquarters
the main office of a company
"Our HQ is in London."
branch
a local office away from HQ
"We have a branch in Bangkok."
department
a division of a company (e.g. Sales, IT)
"I work in the marketing department."
colleague
someone you work with
"This is my colleague, Tom."
client
a customer (usually business)
"She's meeting a new client."
supplier
a company that provides goods/services
"We have a new supplier."

Nationalities & Countries

Country Nationality Example
Thailand Thai She's Thai. / She's from Thailand.
Japan Japanese He's Japanese.
UK / Britain British She's British. / She's from the UK.
USA / America American He's American.
France French She's French.
Germany German He's German.
China Chinese She's Chinese.
Korea Korean He's Korean.
Key pattern: Use be + nationality as an adjective: "I am Thai."
Or use from + country: "I'm from Thailand." — Both are correct and common!
💬 Conversation Practice — 3 Scenarios ▼
🏢 Scenario 1 — Meeting at a conference
A
Hello, I'm David Chen. Nice to meet you.
B
Nice to meet you too. I'm Cat. I'm from Bangkok.
A
Great! What do you do, Cat?
B
I'm a marketing manager at TechLine. And you?
A
I'm a sales director at Global Connect. Here's my business card.
B
Thank you! Here's mine. Let's stay in touch.
💡 Useful phrases: "Nice to meet you." · "Here's my card." · "Let's stay in touch."
🗣️ Your turn: Practise this dialogue with your teacher. Then replace the names and jobs with your own information!
📞 Scenario 2 — Introducing a colleague
A
Cat, this is James. He's our new IT consultant.
B
Hi James, I'm Cat. I'm in the marketing department.
A
Nice to meet you, Cat. Are you from Bangkok?
B
Yes, I am. And you? Where are you from?
A
I'm British. I'm from London originally, but I live here now.
💡 Key structure: "This is [name]. He's/She's [job]." — great for introductions!
🗣️ Your turn: Introduce your teacher to an imaginary colleague. Include: name, job, department, country.
📋 Scenario 3 — Exchanging contact details
A
Do you have an email address I can reach you on?
B
Yes, it's cat.smith@techline.com. Do you have a business card?
A
I don't have one today, sorry! But here's my mobile number.
B
Great. I'll call you next week to arrange a meeting.
💡 Formal vs informal: "Can I have your contact details?" (formal) · "What's your number?" (informal)
🗣️ Your turn: Ask your teacher for their "business" contact details. Use the phrases: Do you have...? Here's my... I'll call you...
🌟 Idioms — Business Networking Language ▼

These idioms are commonly used in business introductions and networking situations.

🧊 break the ice
To start a conversation in an awkward or new social situation.
"He told a joke to break the ice at the conference."
📞 touch base
To make brief contact with someone; to check in.
"Let's touch base next week about the project."
📄 on the same page
To have the same understanding or agreement about something.
"Before we start, let's make sure we're on the same page."
🤝 put a face to a name
To finally meet someone you've only communicated with before.
"It's so nice to put a face to a name — we've emailed a lot!"
🏃 hit the ground running
To start something quickly and energetically from the beginning.
"She's a new employee but she really hit the ground running."
🃏 keep your cards close to your chest
To not share too much information about your plans.
"In negotiations, it's smart to keep your cards close to your chest."
🗣️ Speaking practice: Try using one idiom in a sentence about your own job or company. For example: "In my job, I often need to break the ice with new clients."
🔗 Phrasal Verbs — Phone & Communication ▼

These phrasal verbs are essential for business communication — phone calls, emails, and meetings.

Phrasal Verb Meaning Example Common context
call back Return a phone call to someone "I'll call you back in 10 minutes." Phone calls, meetings
get through Successfully reach someone by phone "I can't get through to the manager." Phone calls
pass on Give a message or information to someone else "Can you pass on a message to Lisa?" Offices, phone messages
fill in Complete a form with required information "Please fill in this registration form." Admin, registration
set up Arrange or organise something (meeting, account) "Can we set up a meeting for Thursday?" Meetings, appointments
look up Find information (in a book, online) "I'll look up her email address for you." Research, contacts
⚠️ Separable phrasal verbs: Some can be split: "Call me back" or "Call back me"? ✅ Both OK with pronouns: "Call me back." ✅ With nouns: "Call back David" or "Call David back."
Fill in the blanks: 1. I called but I couldn't _____ through. (get) 2. Can you _____ a message on to Sarah? (pass) 3. I'll _____ you back this afternoon. (call) 4. Please _____ in this form at reception. (fill) 5. Let's _____ up a meeting for next week. (set)
