🔗 Phrasal Verbs click to expand
Intermediate phrasal verbs for everyday situations — learn the meaning, usage, and practise in context!
  • 🔼
    bring up to mention a topic in conversation
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Bring up means to introduce a subject or topic in a conversation. It can also mean to raise a child.

    ✅ "She brought up an interesting point about the new policy." ✅ "Don't bring up money at the dinner table — it's awkward!" ⚠️ Also: "He was brought up in Bangkok." (= raised as a child)

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak Bring up a topic from the news this week. Use "I wanted to bring up..."
    • Fill in "Can I _____ something? I heard we're getting a new manager."
    • Tip "Bring up" ≠ "bring out." Bring up = mention. Bring out = produce/reveal.
  • 📤
    put off to delay or postpone something
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Put off means to delay doing something until a later time. (Also: to discourage someone.)

    ✅ "I keep putting off cleaning my apartment." ✅ "The meeting was put off until Thursday." ⚠️ "The smell put me off eating." (= discouraged me)

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak What do you always put off doing? Why?
    • Fill in "Let's not _____ this decision any longer. We need to act now."
    • Tip Synonym: "postpone." "Put off" sounds more natural in casual speech.
  • 🔍
    figure out to understand or find a solution
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Figure out means to understand something after thinking about it, or to find a solution to a problem.

    ✅ "I can't figure out how to use this app." ✅ "We finally figured out the problem with the system." ✅ "Have you figured out your plans for the weekend?"

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak Tell me about a time you had to figure something out on your own.
    • Game Riddle time! Try to figure out the answer: "I have hands but no arms. What am I?" (a clock)
    • Tip "Figure out" is separable: "figure it out" ✅ / "figure out it" ❌
  • 🤝
    get along (with) to have a good relationship with someone
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Get along with someone = to have a friendly, positive relationship with them. Also "get on with" (British English).

    ✅ "Do you get along with your neighbours?" ✅ "We didn't get along at first, but now we're good friends." ⚠️ "How are you getting along?" = How are things going? (informal)

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak Tell me about someone you really get along with. What makes the relationship work?
    • Fill in "I _____ well with most of my coworkers, but my boss is a bit difficult."
    • Tip Opposite: "don't get along with" or "clash with."
  • 🌱
    run out of to have no more of something left
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Run out of means to have used all of something so that none remains.

    ✅ "I ran out of time during the exam." ✅ "We've run out of coffee — can you buy some?" ✅ "Don't run out of patience with me!"

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak Have you ever run out of something at a really bad moment? Tell me what happened.
    • Fill in "The printer stopped because we _____ ink halfway through the document."
    • Tip "Run out of" is inseparable — always keep it together. You cannot put the object in the middle.
  • 🔄
    come across to find unexpectedly; to give an impression
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Come across has two main meanings: 1) to find/encounter something by chance; 2) to give a certain impression to others.

    ✅ "I came across an interesting article online yesterday." ✅ "She comes across as very confident in meetings." ⚠️ Watch tone: "He came across as rude" ≠ he intended to be rude.

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak How do you think you come across when you first meet someone? Confident? Shy?
    • Speak Tell me something interesting you came across recently — online, in a book, on TV.
    • Tip For the impression meaning: "come across as + adjective." E.g. "come across as friendly."
  • 💡
    look into to investigate or research something
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Look into means to examine, investigate, or research something more carefully.

    ✅ "I'll look into the problem and get back to you." ✅ "We should look into cheaper flight options." ✅ "The police are looking into the incident."

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak What's something you've been wanting to look into lately? (A hobby, a place, a topic?)
    • Fill in "I'm going to _____ online courses for digital marketing — they seem useful."
    • Tip "Look into" ≠ "look at." Look at = observe. Look into = investigate/research.
  • ✂️
    cut down on to reduce the amount of something
    Phrasal ▼

    📘 Meaning

    Cut down on means to reduce how much you do or use something, usually for health or practical reasons.

    ✅ "I'm trying to cut down on sugar this month." ✅ "The company needs to cut down on expenses." ✅ "I've cut down on social media — I feel much better!"

    🎯 Practice

    • Speak Is there anything you'd like to cut down on in your life? Why?
    • Game Take turns: "I want to cut down on ___" and explain why.
    • Tip Stronger version: "cut out" = stop completely. "I cut out sugar entirely." vs. "I cut down on sugar."

"hit the nail on the head"
about being exactly right
To describe, explain, or identify something exactly and correctly.
"You really hit the nail on the head with that analysis — that's exactly the issue."
tap ▸
"under the weather"
about feeling ill
Feeling slightly sick or not your best — usually a mild illness like a cold or headache.
"I'm feeling a bit under the weather today, so I might skip the gym."
tap ▸
"on the fence"
about not deciding yet
To be undecided between two choices; unable to make up your mind yet.
"I'm still on the fence about changing jobs — the pay is good but the work is boring."
tap ▸
"the ball is in your court"
about who must act next
It is now your responsibility to take the next action or make the next decision.
"I've sent you my proposal — the ball is in your court now."
tap ▸
"break the ice"
about starting a conversation
To do or say something to make people feel more comfortable in a new or awkward situation.
"He told a joke to break the ice at the start of the meeting."
tap ▸
"bite off more than you can chew"
about taking on too much
To try to do more than you are able to handle successfully.
"I agreed to do five projects at once and now I think I've bitten off more than I can chew."
tap ▸
"once in a blue moon"
about something rare
Something that happens very rarely or almost never.
"I only eat fast food once in a blue moon — maybe twice a year."
tap ▸
"get the hang of it"
about learning a skill
To gradually learn how to do something after practising for a while.
"Driving a manual car is tricky at first, but you'll get the hang of it."

    Idiom Match-Up vocabulary · reading
    Activity ▼

    🎯 How to Play

🗣️ Discussion & Opinion Questions

Follow-up: Has your answer changed as you've gotten older? What matters more now?
🌍 Culture & Travel
What's one thing about your culture that you think others might find surprising or unusual?
Follow-up: What cultural differences have you noticed when meeting foreigners?
⚖️ Agree or Disagree
Share your opinion and try to speak for at least 1 minute on each topic.
"Working from home is better than working in an office."
✅ Agree No commute, flexible schedule, better focus, more comfortable environment
❌ Disagree Harder to collaborate, distractions at home, harder to separate work & personal life
"It's too late to learn a language fluently after age 30."
✅ Agree Brain plasticity decreases, accent harder to lose, less time for immersion
❌ Disagree Adults learn more systematically, motivation is stronger, many late-learner success stories
"Money is the most important factor when choosing a job."
✅ Agree Financial security, quality of life, ability to support family and personal goals
❌ Disagree Job satisfaction, mental health, career growth, and sense of purpose matter more
🎭 Role-Play Scenarios
Practice real-world English in context — choose a scenario and act it out with the teacher!
☎️ Calling to Complain
You ordered a product online two weeks ago but it still hasn't arrived. Call the customer service line to find out what happened and ask for a solution.

• Explain your situation and order details

• Ask politely: "I was wondering if you could..."

• Ask for a refund or a replacement

• Stay calm and professional even if frustrated

🍽️ Restaurant Mix-Up
You are at a restaurant and the waiter brought the wrong dish — and it contains something you are allergic to. Talk to the waiter and manager to resolve the situation.

• Politely point out the mistake

• Express your concern about the allergy

• Ask how long the correct order will take

• Ask about compensation (discount, free drink)

💼 Asking for a Raise
You have been at your company for two years and you feel you deserve a higher salary. Practice how to bring this up professionally with your manager.

• Start with: "I wanted to bring something up..."

• Give specific reasons (achievements, hard work)

• State your request clearly but politely

• Respond if the manager says "let me look into it"

📝 Teacher Notes
About Wacha
  • Adult intermediate learner — comfortable with basic grammar but needs more natural, fluent expression.
  • Good listener; responds well to discussion-based activities. Benefits from being asked follow-up questions to extend answers.
  • Key areas to develop: phrasal verbs in context, using idioms naturally, and linking ideas when speaking (using "however," "on the other hand," "having said that...").
  • Can be reserved about making mistakes — offer lots of encouragement and model corrections gently without interrupting mid-sentence.
  • Responds well to real-world scenarios (roleplay, current events). Avoid overly academic grammar-only exercises.
  • Goals: Hold confident, natural conversations in English for work, travel, and everyday life situations.
