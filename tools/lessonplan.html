Adult Speaking Practice

Master advanced grammar and perfect your pronunciation

Part 1: Grammar for Speaking

Conditionals

Zero Conditional (General Truth)
If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
Structure: If + present simple, present simple. Used for facts and general truths.
First Conditional (Real Future)
If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.
Structure: If + present simple, will + infinitive. Used for real possibilities in the future.
Second Conditional (Hypothetical Present)
If I had more time, I would learn Japanese.
Structure: If + past simple, would + infinitive. Used for imaginary or unlikely situations.
Third Conditional (Hypothetical Past)
If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.
Structure: If + past perfect, would have + past participle. Used for imaginary situations in the past.

Practice Questions - Conditionals

Practice answering these questions out loud. Focus on using the correct conditional form.
What will you do if you have free time this weekend?
Use First Conditional: "If I have free time, I will..."
What would you do if you won the lottery?
Use Second Conditional: "If I won the lottery, I would..."
What would you have done differently in your career if you could go back in time?
Use Third Conditional: "If I could go back, I would have..."
What happens to your mood if you don't get enough sleep?
Use Zero Conditional: "If I don't get enough sleep, I feel/become..."

Speaking Activity: Chain Conditionals

Create a chain of consequences. Start with: "If I wake up late tomorrow..." and continue the story using different conditional forms.

Example: "If I wake up late tomorrow, I will miss my bus. If I miss my bus, I would have to take a taxi. If I had set my alarm earlier, I wouldn't have this problem."

Modal Verbs

Can / Could (Ability & Permission)
I can speak three languages. Could you help me with this?
Can = present ability/permission. Could = past ability or polite request.
May / Might (Possibility)
It may rain later. She might be running late.
Both express possibility. May is slightly more formal.
Must / Have to (Obligation)
You must wear a seatbelt. I have to finish this report today.
Must = strong personal obligation. Have to = external obligation or necessity.
Should / Ought to (Advice)
You should see a doctor. We ought to leave soon.
Both give advice or recommendations. Should is more common.
Would (Hypothetical & Polite)
I would love to travel more. Would you like some coffee?
Used for hypothetical situations and polite offers/requests.
Will (Future & Willingness)
I'll be there at 5 PM. I'll help you with that.
Expresses future actions and willingness to do something.

Practice Questions - Modal Verbs

Answer these questions using appropriate modal verbs.
What are three things you can do well?
Use "can": "I can cook, I can drive, I can..."
What should people do to stay healthy?
Use "should": "People should exercise regularly, they should..."
What do you have to do before you go to bed every night?
Use "have to" or "must": "I have to brush my teeth, I must..."
What might you do this weekend?
Use "might" or "may": "I might visit friends, I may..."
What would you do if you could live anywhere in the world?
Use "would": "I would move to..., I would..."

Role Play Activity: Give Advice

Imagine your friend has these problems. Give advice using modal verbs (should, could, might want to, ought to).

Problems:
  • I'm always tired at work.
  • I want to improve my English but don't have time.
  • I'm nervous about my job interview tomorrow.
  • I can't decide whether to buy a car or save money.

Part 2: Pronunciation Practice

Difficult Phrases to Master

Practice saying these challenging phrases. Click each one when you feel confident pronouncing it correctly.

"The sixth sick sheikh's sixth sheep's sick"
Focus on the "s" and "sh" sounds. Take it slowly!
"She sells seashells by the seashore"
Practice the "s" and "sh" distinction clearly.
"Red lorry, yellow lorry"
The "r" and "l" sounds - say it 3 times fast!
"I thought a thought, but the thought I thought wasn't the thought I thought I thought"
Focus on the "th" sound in different positions.
"How can a clam cram in a clean cream can?"
Master the "cl" and "cr" consonant clusters.
"Specifically, the Pacific Ocean is terrific"
Practice word stress and the "f" sound.
"Unique New York, you need New York, you know you need unique New York"
Focus on "you" and "new" - don't blend them!
"The rural juror ruled in favor of rural architecture"
The "r" sound in "rural" - very challenging!
"Three free throws"
Clear "th" and "r" pronunciation needed.
"Eleven benevolent elephants"
Watch the "l" and "v" sounds clearly.

Common Pronunciation Challenges for Adults

TH Sounds (/θ/ and /ð/)
Think, thank, this, that, mother, father
Voiceless "th" (think) vs. voiced "th" (this). Put tongue between teeth!
R vs. L Sounds
Right/light, read/lead, rice/lice, collect/correct
R: curl tongue back. L: touch tongue to roof of mouth behind teeth.
V vs. B Sounds
Very/berry, vest/best, vote/boat, event/abundant
V: upper teeth touch lower lip. B: lips together.
Final Consonant Sounds
Bad, bag, back, bat (all end differently!)
Don't drop final consonants - pronounce them clearly.
Consonant Clusters
Street, strong, asked, twelfth, sixths
Practice multiple consonants together without adding vowels.
Word Stress
PHOtograph, phoTOgraphy, photoGRAPHic
Stress changes meaning and rhythm - very important!
