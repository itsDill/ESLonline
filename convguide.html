20 Essential Conversational English Expressions

Master these everyday expressions to sound more natural and confident in English conversations!

Natural Everyday Conversations

Conversational English is full of expressions and idioms that might not appear in textbooks. Learning these phrases will help you understand native speakers better and express yourself more naturally. Click on each expression to learn its meaning and see examples of how it's used in real conversations.

1
How's it going? Greeting

Meaning: A casual way to ask someone how they are doing or feeling. Similar to "How are you?" but more informal.

When to use: In casual settings with friends, colleagues, or people you know well.

Alice: Hey, Mark! How's it going?

Mark: Not bad, thanks! Just busy with work. How about you?

Alice: Pretty good! I just finished a big project yesterday.

Note: Common responses include "Good," "Not bad," "Can't complain," "Pretty good," or sometimes people might share what's happening in their life.

2
What's up? Greeting

Meaning: A very casual greeting that can mean "Hello" or "What's happening?"

When to use: With close friends or in very informal situations. Not appropriate in formal settings.

Jack: What's up, Carlos?

Carlos: Hey! Not much, just chilling. You?

Jack: Same. Thinking about grabbing some lunch soon.

Note: Common responses include "Not much," "Nothing much," or briefly mentioning what you're currently doing.

3
No way! Reaction

Meaning: An expression of disbelief or surprise. Equivalent to "I don't believe it!" or "That's incredible!"

When to use: When someone tells you surprising news or information that's hard to believe.

Sarah: Did you hear that Tom won the lottery last weekend?

Mike: No way! How much did he win?

Sarah: About $50,000!

Mike: That's awesome! Good for him.

Note: The tone of voice is important - it should sound enthusiastic and surprised.

4
Hang out Socializing

Meaning: To spend time with someone in a relaxed, informal setting. No specific activity is implied.

When to use: When suggesting or asking about spending casual time with friends.

Jennifer: Do you want to hang out this weekend?

David: Sure! What did you have in mind?

Jennifer: Nothing specific. We could just grab coffee and catch up.

Note: This is much more casual than "meeting up" or having an appointment.

5
I'm not sure about that Opinion

Meaning: A polite way to disagree or express doubt without being confrontational.

When to use: When you want to express disagreement in a subtle, non-aggressive way.

Kevin: I think this restaurant has the best pizza in town.

Lisa: I'm not sure about that. Have you tried the new place on Main Street?

Kevin: No, is it good?

Lisa: It's amazing! We should go there sometime.

Note: This phrase helps maintain a positive conversation while still expressing your different opinion.

6
That's awesome! Reaction

Meaning: An enthusiastic way to express that something is very good or impressive.

When to use: When reacting to good news or impressive achievements.

Rachel: I just got accepted to the university I applied to!

Daniel: That's awesome! Congratulations! When do you start?

Rachel: This fall. I'm really excited about it.

Daniel: You should be! It's a great achievement.

Note: This is more enthusiastic than simply saying "That's good" or "That's nice."

7
Could you give me a hand? Request

Meaning: To ask for help or assistance with something.

When to use: When you need help with a task or situation.

Paul: Could you give me a hand with these boxes? They're quite heavy.

Maria: Sure, no problem. Where do you want them?

Paul: Just in the living room for now. Thanks!

Note: This is more casual than "Could you help me?" but still polite.

8
Keep in touch Socializing

Meaning: To maintain contact or communication with someone.

When to use: When saying goodbye to someone you want to stay connected with.

Emily: It was great seeing you again after so long!

Jason: You too! Let's keep in touch this time.

Emily: Definitely! I'll text you when I'm back in town.

Note: This suggests maintaining communication, not necessarily meeting in person again soon.

9
I see what you mean Opinion

Meaning: To understand someone's perspective or point of view.

When to use: When acknowledging someone's opinion, even if you might not fully agree.

Robert: I think we should postpone the meeting until everyone can attend.

Anna: I see what you mean, but we're under a tight deadline.

Robert: That's true. Maybe we could have a brief meeting now and a full one later?

Anna: That sounds like a good compromise.

Note: This shows respect for the other person's perspective while allowing you to add your own thoughts.

10
You're kidding! Reaction

Meaning: An expression of disbelief or surprise, similar to "Are you serious?"

When to use: When you hear something surprising or hard to believe.

Brian: I just ran into our high school teacher at the grocery store.

Tina: You're kidding! Mr. Peterson? I thought he moved to Florida.

Brian: Nope, he's still here. He asked about you, actually.

Tina: Wow, that's really unexpected!

Note: This doesn't literally mean you think the person is joking, but rather expresses surprise.

11
Do you mind if...? Request

Meaning: A polite way to ask permission before doing something.

When to use: When you want to be courteous about a request or action.

Nicole: Do you mind if I open the window? It's getting a bit warm in here.

John: Not at all, go ahead.

Nicole: Thanks!

Note: The correct response to "Do you mind if...?" is "No" if you're okay with it and "Yes" or "Actually, I do" if you're not okay with it - which can be confusing for ESL learners!

12
Long time no see Greeting

Meaning: A friendly greeting used when you haven't seen someone for a while.

When to use: When reuniting with a friend or acquaintance after a long absence.

Michael: Hey, Jessica! Long time no see!

Jessica: Michael! It's been ages! How have you been?

Michael: I've been great! How about you? What are you up to these days?

Note: This phrase is grammatically incorrect but widely used in casual conversation.

13
Let's catch up Socializing

Meaning: To suggest spending time together to update each other on what's been happening in your lives.

When to use: When you want to reconnect with someone you haven't seen in a while.

Alex: It's been forever since we talked properly. Let's catch up soon!

Olivia: Definitely! Are you free for coffee this weekend?

Alex: Sunday would work for me. How about you?

Note: This implies a more purposeful conversation where you'll share updates about your life, not just casual hanging out.
