📋 Main Modal Verbs

Modal Common Uses Example
can ability, permission, possibility I can swim. / Can I leave early?
could past ability, polite requests, possibility I could run fast when I was 10. / Could you help me?
may permission, possibility You may go now. / It may rain later.
might weaker possibility We might be late.
must strong obligation or logical deduction You must wear a seatbelt. / That must be her car.
shall (formal) future (esp. "I/we"), offers, suggestions Shall we begin?
should advice, recommendation, expectation You should study tonight.
will future, willingness, certainty I will call you tomorrow.
would polite speech, hypothetical, habitual past I would go if I had time. / Would you like tea?

🔧 Semi-Modal Verbs

Semi-modal Meaning Example
have to obligation (external rule) I have to wear a uniform.
need to necessity You need to study more.
ought to moral duty / strong advice You ought to apologize.
used to past habit or state I used to live in London.
be going to future intention I'm going to call her later.

Teaching Tip:

A fun way to introduce modals is by function group, e.g.:

🟢 Ability: can, could
🟠 Advice: should, ought to
🔴 Obligation: must, have to
🟡 Permission: can, may
⚪ Possibility: may, might, could
🔵 Politeness / Hypotheticals: would

Modal verbs are special auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, and other attitudes toward actions. They modify the main verb to add layers of meaning!

📚 What Are Modal Verbs?

Essential auxiliary verbs that add nuance and meaning to your English!

Modal Verbs Overview

Foundation
🤔 Can you guess what makes modal verbs special?
What are Modal Verbs? Special auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, and other attitudes toward actions.
The Main Modals:
Can, Could: "I can swim" (ability)
May, Might: "It may rain" (possibility)
Will, Would: "I will help" (future/politeness)
Shall, Should: "You should study" (advice)
Must, Ought to: "You must stop" (obligation)
Key Rules: Never take -s, -ed, or -ing • Always followed by base verb • No "to" (except "ought to")
💡 Tip: Modal verbs are essential for polite communication and expressing uncertainty!

🟢 CAN / COULD

Ability, Permission, and Polite Requests

1. CAN

Ability Permission
🤔 Can you guess what "CAN" means and when to use it?
Meaning: Expresses ability, possibility, and informal permission
Examples:
Ability: "I can speak three languages."
Permission: "You can borrow my car."
Possibility: "It can get very cold in winter."
Requests: "Can you help me with this?"
When to use: Present ability, informal situations, general possibilities
💡 Tip: Negative: "cannot" or "can't" - "I can't swim."

2. COULD

Past Ability Polite
🤔 How is "COULD" different from "CAN"?
Meaning: Past ability, polite requests, future possibilities, suggestions
Examples:
Past ability: "I could run faster when I was younger."
Polite request: "Could you please send me that file?"
Possibility: "It could rain tomorrow."
Suggestion: "We could try that new restaurant."
When to use: More polite than "can", hypothetical situations, past abilities
💡 Tip: "Could you help?" is more polite than "Can you help?"

🟡 MAY / MIGHT

Formal Permission and Possibility

3. MAY

Formal Permission
🤔 When do we use "MAY" instead of "CAN"?
Meaning: Formal permission and moderate possibility (50% certainty)
Examples:
Formal permission: "May I come in?"
Giving permission: "You may leave when finished."
Possibility: "It may rain later today."
Formal wishes: "May you have a wonderful journey."
When to use: Formal situations, official permissions, 50% possibility
💡 Tip: More formal than "can" - use in professional settings

4. MIGHT

Lower Possibility
🤔 How is "MIGHT" different from "MAY"?
Meaning: Lower possibility (30-40% certainty), polite suggestions
Examples:
Lower possibility: "I might go to the party, but I'm not sure."
Polite suggestion: "You might want to try this approach."
Hypothetical past: "If I had studied, I might have passed."
Soft criticism: "You might have told me earlier."
When to use: Less certain than "may", softening statements
💡 Tip: "Might" is less certain than "may" - shows more doubt

🔵 WILL / WOULD

Future Actions and Polite Requests

5. WILL

Future Decisions
🤔 When do we use "WILL"?
Meaning: Future actions, promises, spontaneous decisions, predictions
Examples:
Simple future: "I will see you tomorrow."
Spontaneous decision: "I'll answer the phone."
Promise: "I will call you later."
Prediction: "She will do well in the exam."
When to use: Future events, making promises, immediate decisions
💡 Tip: Contracted form: "I'll" - very common in speech!

6. WOULD

Polite Hypothetical
🤔 How is "WOULD" different from "WILL"?
Meaning: Polite requests, hypothetical situations, past habits, preferences
Examples:
Polite request: "Would you please close the window?"
Hypothetical: "If I won the lottery, I would travel."
Past habit: "I would visit my grandparents every summer."
Preference: "I would prefer coffee to tea."
When to use: More polite than "will" for requests, conditional sentences
💡 Tip: "Would you help?" is more polite than "Will you help?"

🟠 SHALL / SHOULD

Formal Future and Advice

7. SHOULD

Advice Expectation
🤔 When do we use "SHOULD"?
Meaning: Advice, recommendations, expectations, mild obligation
Examples:
Advice: "You should see a doctor."
Expectation: "She should be here by now."
Obligation: "Students should complete their homework."
Regret: "I should have studied harder."
When to use: Giving advice, expressing what's expected or right
💡 Tip: Softer than "must" - suggests rather than commands

🔴 MUST

Strong Obligation and Logical Deduction

8. MUST

Strong Obligation
🤔 What makes "MUST" different from "SHOULD"?
Meaning: Strong obligation, necessity, logical deduction (95% certainty)
Examples:
Strong obligation: "You must wear a seatbelt."
Necessity: "I must finish this today."
Strong advice: "You must try this restaurant!"
Logical deduction: "She must be sleeping." (90%+ certain)
When to use: Rules, laws, strong recommendations, logical conclusions
💡 Critical: "mustn't" = prohibited, "don't have to" = not necessary!

🔧 Semi-Modal Verbs

Verb phrases that function like modal verbs

9. HAVE TO

External Obligation
🤔 How is "HAVE TO" different from "MUST"?
Meaning: External obligation or necessity (rules, laws, circumstances)
Examples:
External rule: "I have to wear a uniform at work."
Necessity: "We have to leave now or we'll miss the train."
Past obligation: "I had to study all night."
Future obligation: "You will have to register first."
When to use: External rules, necessities imposed by others or circumstances
💡 Key difference: "MUST" = internal decision, "HAVE TO" = external requirement

10. NEED TO

Necessity
🤔 When do we use "NEED TO"?
Meaning: Personal necessity or requirement
Examples:
Personal need: "You need to study more for the exam."
Practical necessity: "I need to buy groceries."
Advice: "You need to see a doctor about that cough."
Negative: "You don't need to bring anything."
When to use: Personal requirements, practical necessities, strong advice
💡 Tip: More personal than "have to" - focuses on individual needs

11. OUGHT TO

Moral Duty
🤔 What makes "OUGHT TO" special?
Meaning: Moral duty, strong advice, what is right or proper
Examples:
Moral duty: "You ought to apologize for what you said."
Strong advice: "You ought to take that job offer."
Expectation: "She ought to be here by now."
Past regret: "I ought to have studied harder."
When to use: Moral obligations, strong recommendations, formal contexts
💡 Note: More formal than "should" - emphasizes moral duty

12. USED TO

Past Habit
🤔 What does "USED TO" express?
Meaning: Past habits or states that are no longer true
Examples:
Past habit: "I used to play tennis every weekend."
Past state: "This used to be a quiet neighborhood."
Negative: "I didn't use to like spicy food."
Question: "Did you use to live in Paris?"
When to use: Describing past situations that have changed
💡 Important: Different from "be used to" (accustomed to) and "get used to" (become accustomed)

13. BE GOING TO

Future Intention
🤔 When do we use "BE GOING TO" instead of "WILL"?
Meaning: Future intentions, plans, predictions based on evidence
Examples:
Future plan: "I'm going to call her later."
Intention: "We're going to move to a bigger house."
Evidence-based prediction: "Look at those clouds - it's going to rain."
Near future: "I'm going to have lunch now."
When to use: Pre-planned actions, intentions, predictions with evidence
💡 Key difference: "Going to" = planned, "Will" = spontaneous decision

💡 Teaching Tips & Strategies

Effective ways to teach modal verbs

Function-Based Learning

Teaching Strategy
🤔 What's the best way to group modal verbs for teaching?

Function-Based Grouping Strategy

🟢 ABILITY GROUP
  • can - present ability
  • could - past ability
  • be able to - all tenses
"I can swim" → "I could swim" → "I will be able to swim"
🟠 ADVICE GROUP
  • should - general advice
  • ought to - moral duty
  • had better - strong advice
"You should study" → "You ought to help" → "You'd better hurry"
🔴 OBLIGATION GROUP
  • must - strong internal
  • have to - external rule
  • need to - necessity
"I must go" → "I have to work" → "I need to eat"
🟡 PERMISSION GROUP
  • can - informal
  • may - formal
  • could - polite
"Can I go?" → "May I leave?" → "Could I borrow this?"
⚪ POSSIBILITY GROUP
  • may - 50% chance
  • might - 30% chance
  • could - general possibility
"It may rain" → "It might rain" → "It could rain"
🔵 POLITENESS GROUP
  • would - polite requests
  • could - polite questions
  • might - very polite
"Would you help?" → "Could you try?" → "Might I suggest?"
🎯 Progressive Teaching Sequence:
  1. Week 1: Start with can/can't for ability and permission
  2. Week 2: Add should/shouldn't for advice
  3. Week 3: Introduce must/mustn't vs have to/don't have to
  4. Week 4: Teach will/would for future and politeness
  5. Week 5: Cover may/might/could for possibility
  6. Week 6: Review and practice all functions together
