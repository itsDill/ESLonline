English Vowel Sounds

Master the foundation of English pronunciation with these essential vowel sounds.

Short Vowels

Short vowels are generally produced with less tension and are shorter in duration. They're often found in stressed closed syllables (syllables that end with a consonant).

Sound IPA Symbol Examples Description
Short "a" /æ/ cat, map, hat Open mouth, tongue flat, slightly tensed
Short "e" /ɛ/ bed, pet, red Medium open mouth, tongue slightly raised
Short "i" /ɪ/ sit, lip, fish Slightly smiling position, relaxed
Short "o" /ɒ/ or /ɑ/ hot, pot, top Open rounded mouth, tongue back and down
Short "u" /ʌ/ but, cup, luck Medium open mouth, relaxed tongue
Practice Tips
  • Exaggerate the mouth position at first to feel the correct placement.
  • Practice minimal pairs that contrast short vowels, such as "sit" vs. "set".
  • Record yourself and compare with native speakers.
  • Use a mirror to check your mouth position.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing /æ/ and /ɛ/ (e.g., saying "bed" like "bad").
  • Making short vowels too long or tense.
  • Not relaxing enough for /ɪ/ (making it sound like "ee" as in "deep").
  • Using the wrong mouth shape for /ʌ/.

Long Vowels

Long vowels are produced with more tension and, as the name suggests, longer duration. They often appear in open syllables (syllables that end with a vowel).

Sound IPA Symbol Examples Description
Long "a" /eɪ/ day, make, pain Begins with mouth slightly open, closes to a smile
Long "e" /iː/ see, heat, read Wide smile, tongue high in front
Long "i" /aɪ/ like, time, high Starts open, moves to a smile
Long "o" /oʊ/ go, boat, show Round lips, moving from open to closed
Long "u" /uː/ blue, food, moon Lips tightly rounded, tongue high in back
Long "a" vs Short "a"
day vs bat
Long "e" vs Short "i"
beat vs bit
Practice Tips
  • Pay attention to the movement of your mouth when producing long vowels.
  • Practice extending the duration of long vowels without changing quality.
  • Use hand gestures to indicate the change in sound quality (for diphthongs).
  • Remember that spelling doesn't always match pronunciation.
